Policing Insecurity

A Visual Analysis of State Building in Transitions to Peace

Introduction

"In environments of seemingly intractable conflict, how should we understand the role of state capacity building and security-sector reform in transitions to peace?"

Key Question

This research examines the paradoxical relationship between police capacity building and peace transitions in conflict-affected states.

Research Context

"The study focuses on eastern DR Congo, where state-building efforts have been ongoing for decades."

Historical Background

Understanding the complex interplay between state institutions and local security dynamics is crucial for effective peacebuilding.

The Core Argument

"Enhancing the coercive capacity of the police can entrench a wartime political order that makes peace more elusive; when police deploy the image of the state toward destabilizing ends they reinforce the institutions of everyday war, undermining the stability a monopoly on violence is intended to build."

Implications

This finding challenges conventional wisdom about the relationship between state security apparatus and peace transitions.

Key Findings

State Security Apparatus

"Prevailing wisdom suggests that a strong state security apparatus mitigates cyclical violence and aids in transitions to predictable, rule-governed behavior."

Police Brutality

"Growing attention to police brutality in institutionalized democracies calls this assumption into question."

Local Dynamics

The study reveals how local power dynamics can co-opt state security institutions for destabilizing purposes.

Institutional Reform

Traditional approaches to security sector reform may inadvertently reinforce existing power structures.

Research Methodology

The study employs a multi-method approach combining ethnographic research, interviews, and institutional analysis.

Data Collection

Extensive fieldwork in eastern DR Congo provided rich qualitative data on security sector dynamics.

Conclusion

"The study of war making and state making in eastern DR Congo reveals how reform efforts intended to bolster the state's monopoly on violence frequently fail to curb the unrest they seek to disrupt."

Policy Implications

These findings suggest the need for more nuanced approaches to security sector reform in conflict-affected states.
